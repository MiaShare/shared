Internal — engineering brief

Callers can't talk over the voice agent

When the AI is reading an answer back to the caller, the caller can't interrupt it — not because the system is ignoring them, but because the system literally cannot hear them. The voice-recognition layer is being fed the AI's own voice instead of the caller's.

Branch lum-3088-voice-interruption-logic · 2026-05-15

The bug in 30 seconds

  1. 1 Caller asks the AI a question. The AI looks up an answer and starts reading it back.
  2. 2 Caller tries to interject — "stop", "wait", "actually, never mind".
  3. 3 The AI keeps talking. The caller has to either wait, repeat themselves louder, or hang up.

We have hard evidence that this happens because the AI's own voice is being delivered into the same audio channel the system uses to listen for the caller. While the AI is talking, that channel is saturated with the AI's voice and the caller's voice never reaches the speech-recognition layer.

How we proved it

The two earlier test calls gave us strong circumstantial evidence — Vapi's speech recognizer kept transcribing the AI's own words when the caller was clearly talking — but circumstantial isn't a proof. To pin the bug down we ran a controlled experiment that produced two independent transcripts of the same call, captured at two different points in Vapi's pipeline, and compared them.

The experiment, in five steps

  1. 1
    Turn Vapi's recording feature back on for one test call

    We normally have Vapi-side recording off (Twilio records the carrier leg directly, so storing it twice is wasteful). For this one call we flipped recordingEnabled: true in the assistant config so Vapi would also store the audio it processed internally, broken out by channel.

  2. 2
    Place one staging call, interrupt aggressively

    A 41-second test call where we tried to talk over the AI on every one of its turns — greeting, hold-replies, the actual answer read-back. Wired headset throughout, so acoustic echo from a speakerphone can't be a factor.

  3. 3
    Download what Vapi stored — four audio files plus their internal event log

    Vapi's API returned exactly what their documentation promises: four audio recordings (caller-only, AI-only, mono mix, stereo) plus a second separate artifact — the full chronological log of every event their pipeline emitted during the call, including every partial transcript their speech recognizer produced. Two completely separate representations of the same call, captured at different stages of their pipeline.

  4. 4
    Run a second, independent transcription against Vapi's own recordings

    This is the trick. We took the four WAV files Vapi gave us and ran each one through a completely separate speech-recognition tool (macOS's built-in audio transcription, which has nothing to do with Vapi or Deepgram). That gives us a neutral, ground-truth read of what's actually in each audio file — independent of any decision Vapi's pipeline made.

  5. 5
    Compare the two transcripts, second by second

    For each AI-speaking window in the call, we now have two answers to "what was the caller saying?" — the independent macOS transcription of Vapi's caller-only recording, and Vapi's own recognizer output for that same window. If they agree, the recognizer is doing its job. If they disagree, the recognizer is receiving different audio than the recorder is — and the gap between them tells us exactly where in their pipeline the bug lives.

The audio file Vapi stored contains the caller's question. Vapi's own recognizer, on the same call, transcribed something completely different — the AI's own sentence, word for word.

Where this happens inside Vapi

Vapi's pipeline has two distinct internal stages that both process the same call audio in real time. One stores recordings. The other runs speech recognition. They are reading from different audio sources, and that's the bug.

Left branch — Vapi's recorder

Reads the caller's audio on a clean, separate channel. Produces the four WAV files we downloaded. The caller-only.wav file contains the caller's voice and only the caller's voice. Channel separation here is provably working.

Right branch — Vapi's recognizer

Receives a different audio source — one that has the AI's own outbound voice mixed in. The recognizer transcribes that audio, sees the AI's words during AI turns, decides no caller is speaking, and never tells the rest of the system to stop. This branch is reading from the wrong place.

We don't have visibility into the exact wiring inside Vapi's infrastructure — only into what comes out the API. But the observable behaviour pins the bug down to this one divergence: their recorder and their recognizer are reading from two different audio sources on the same call. Whatever they're doing for the recorder (clean channel isolation) needs to also apply to the recognizer. That's the fix.

Why this confused us for a while

Interruption does work on the AI's opening greeting. That made the bug look intermittent and hard to pin down. The logs explain why: during the greeting, the caller's voice partially bleeds through to the recognition layer enough times that the system eventually catches it. After the greeting, the AI's voice fully dominates the channel and the caller becomes invisible to the system.

We tried five different configuration changes before pulling the per-event log — tightening voice-detection thresholds, swapping the speech-recognition provider, adding noise suppression, switching transcription models. None of them moved the needle, because the problem is upstream of every knob we have access to. The vendor (Vapi) is mixing our AI's voice into the inbound audio stream before any of our settings get to look at it.

Root cause

Vapi (the voice AI platform between Twilio and our AI) has two separate audio pipelines inside its system. The pipeline that records the call keeps the caller and the AI on separate channels — we confirmed this by downloading the per-channel recordings Vapi generated, and the caller's channel contains only the caller's voice, with no AI audio. The pipeline that feeds the speech recognizer does not. It receives the AI's audio mixed in with the caller's, on the same channel.

When the AI is talking, its voice in the mixed stream is loud and continuous; the caller's voice gets drowned out before the recognizer can pick it up. From the recognizer's perspective the caller is silent the whole time the AI is talking, so nothing fires the barge-in detector.

The two pipelines diverging is the bug, and it lives inside Vapi's infrastructure. Vapi's own recording layer proves that the channel separation is technically achievable on the same call — they just don't apply it to the recognizer input.

How we fix it

Three paths, ordered by cost and confidence.

  1. 1

    Vapi support ticket — already filed, now reinforced

    The initial support ticket is in with 8 reproducing call IDs. The new per-channel recording from today's test call adds the missing piece: their own recorder proves the channels can be separated on this exact call; their recognizer just doesn't use the separation. That removes any room for Vapi to argue this is a configuration or carrier issue on our side.

    Cost: hours. Confidence it works: medium — depends on Vapi's responsiveness, but the evidence is now decisive.

  2. 2

    Change how calls reach Vapi

    The mixing happens because of the specific way Twilio currently hands the call to Vapi. There is an alternative — bring our Twilio phone numbers directly into Vapi ownership and let Vapi receive the call without the Twilio "bypass" step in the middle. This removes the audio path where the mixing happens. It's a larger architectural change and would require careful migration of every institution's numbers, but it is the highest-confidence fix if Vapi can't address it on their side.

    Cost: days to a couple of weeks. Confidence it works: high — different audio routing entirely.

  3. 3

    Bring more of the voice pipeline in-house

    If Vapi can't fix this on their side and the architectural change in option 2 doesn't hold up, the longer-term answer is owning the audio handling ourselves — our own server intercepts the Twilio stream, separates caller and AI audio, and forwards only the caller's audio to the recognition service. This is the most flexible and the most expensive; it's a fallback, not a starting point.

    Cost: weeks. Confidence it works: very high — we control every byte.

Recommended next step

File the Vapi support ticket this week with the two staging call IDs and the per-event log evidence attached. In parallel, scope option 2 (the architectural change) so we have a real second option ready if Vapi cannot resolve this on their side within a reasonable window. We do not recommend committing to option 3 yet — it's an order of magnitude more work and we shouldn't take it on until we know the first two paths are exhausted.

In the meantime, the in-product interruption logic we built (clarify-vs-defer classification, suppression of stale answers, bridge phrases) is sound and will take effect the moment the underlying audio routing is fixed. None of that work needs to be redone.

What this is not

  • It is not a regression — this behavior has been present since we first turned on the voice agent. We only just isolated the cause.
  • It is not a code bug in our application. Every layer we own is doing what it should — the recognition layer simply never receives the caller's voice during the AI's read-back.
  • It is not blocking us from shipping the rest of the voice-agent work. The interruption-handling logic we built around it is ready and will start working the moment the audio routing is fixed.
